# Food Chain Magnate — Common Confusion Points & Teaching Notes

> As a boardgame teacher, these are the aspects of FCM that consistently trip up new players and require extra attention when explaining. Organized from "most commonly confused" to "subtler gotchas."

---

## 🔴 Critical Confusion Areas (Almost Everyone Gets Wrong)

### 1. Company Structure Overflow = Total Wipeout

New players almost never expect this: if you commit employees to your work pile during Restructuring and you **cannot legally assign all of them** to Work slots, you don't just lose the extras — **every single employee except your CEO goes On the Beach**. You play the entire round with just your CEO.

**Why it confuses people:** In most games, you'd just discard the extras. Here, the punishment is catastrophic. Players also forget that managers occupy a slot themselves while providing slots — it's easy to miscount.

**Teaching tip:** Use the Work Planning Variant for the first game. Let players lay cards out face-down in their actual slots before committing. Explicitly walk through a "what if you overcommit" scenario during setup.

---

### 2. Demand Must Be FULLY Satisfied — No Partial Sales

A house with 2 burgers and 1 beer demand will **not** eat at your restaurant if you only have 2 burgers. You must have **every single item** the house wants. Having 90% of what they want is the same as having 0%.

**Why it confuses people:** Every other economic game allows partial fulfillment. Players instinctively think "I have most of what they need, surely I get *some* credit." Nope. The customers are absurdly picky.

**Teaching tip:** Emphasize this multiple times. Use the phrase "all or nothing" and repeat it when players start producing food.

---

### 3. Road Route Tracing & Backtracking Rules

The drink collection road route system is where the most rules questions arise:

- Routes start from the **Entrance Corner** of your restaurant, not from anywhere on the restaurant
- Range counts **tile border crossings**, not road squares
- You **can** backtrack through previously-visited road squares, BUT you **cannot** immediately reverse direction (step back onto the square you just came from)
- Each drink source can only be collected from **once per employee per route**, even if you pass it multiple times
- If your entrance is at a tile edge, it costs **1 Range** just to get onto the adjacent tile's road — your route doesn't "start" until you're on a road square

**Why it confuses people:** The no-immediate-backtracking rule is oddly specific. Players mix up "range" (tile crossings) with "movement" (road squares). The entrance-at-tile-edge nuance is deeply counterintuitive.

**Teaching tip:** Walk through the rulebook's drink route example step by step. Physically trace the path with a finger on the map. Make sure players understand that "Range 2" doesn't mean "2 squares."

---

### 4. Distance vs. Range vs. Road Squares — Three Different Things

FCM uses three spatial concepts that sound similar but are mechanically distinct:

| Concept | What it counts | Used for |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| **Distance** | Tile border crossings (shortest path) | Effective price in competition |
| **Range** | Tile border crossings (max on your route) | Employee action reach |
| **Road squares** | Individual grid squares along a road | Tracing the actual path |

**Why it confuses people:** "Distance" and "Range" both count tile crossings but serve different purposes. Players instinctively want to count road squares for everything.

**Teaching tip:** Always say "tile borders" when explaining distance and range. Never say "spaces" or "squares" for these — reserve those words for the road grid.

---

### 5. Marketing Creates Demand AFTER Dinnertime

Marketing campaigns don't add demand counters when placed — they add demand during the **Marketing phase (Phase 6)**, which happens **after Dinnertime (Phase 4)**. This means:

- A campaign placed this round will not generate demand until **after** this round's sales
- The very first demand can only appear after Dinnertime of Round 2 at the earliest
- Dinnertime is always skipped for at least the first 2 rounds

**Why it confuses people:** Players assume "I placed a billboard, now houses want food." The phase order is counterintuitive — you market AFTER dinner, not before.

**Teaching tip:** When explaining the round structure, specifically highlight that Phase 6 (Marketing) comes after Phase 4 (Dinnertime). Say: "You market tonight so that tomorrow people are hungry."

## 🟠 Frequently Misunderstood Mechanics

### 6. Turn Order — More Empty Slots = Pick First

Players with the **most open (empty) Work slots** pick their turn order position first. This is backwards from most games where "doing less" feels like a penalty.

**Why it confuses people:** Intuitively, having more empty slots feels like you're weaker. But in FCM, going first can be hugely powerful for hiring, and leaving slots empty is a legitimate strategic choice.

**Teaching tip:** Frame it as "efficiency" — a lean company gets to choose when they act.

---

### 7. Mandatory Actions — You MUST Use Them

Several employee types have **mandatory** actions. If you play a Pricing Manager, your prices **must** go down, even if you don't want them to. If you play a Waitress, you **must** take the cash bonus (which sounds good until you realize it can inadvertently break the bank against your wishes).

Mandatory actions include:
- Drive-In signs (Local/Regional Manager)
- Price changes (Pricing Manager, Luxuries Manager, Discount Manager)
- Cash bonuses (Waitress, CFO)
- Salary discounts (Recruiting Manager, HR Director)

**Why it confuses people:** Players think "I'll play this employee just for the Work slots, I'll ignore the action." You can't.

**Teaching tip:** When explaining each employee type, explicitly flag mandatory vs. optional. A good rule of thumb: Phase 3 (Working 9-5) actions are optional, but Phase 4-5 (Dinnertime/Payday) actions are mandatory. Exception: Drive-In signs are mandatory in Phase 3.

---

### 8. The Salary Trap — On the Beach Employees Still Cost Money

Employees On the Beach provide no actions and no Work slots, but you **still pay their salary**. New players hire aggressively early, then get buried in salary costs for employees they can't even use.

**Why it confuses people:** "I benched them, why do I have to pay them?" Also, busy Marketeers running campaigns must be paid too, and you **cannot voluntarily fire them**.

**Teaching tip:** Before anyone hires their second employee, remind them: "Every employee with a salary icon costs $5 every round, whether they work or sit on the beach."

---

### 9. Effective Price = Item Price + Distance (But You're Paid Item Price Only)

During competition, the house chooses based on **effective price** (item price + distance), but the winning restaurant is paid only their **item price**. Distance penalizes you in competition but doesn't increase your revenue.

**Why it confuses people:** Players think "I'm further away so I charge more" or "distance affects my income." Neither is true. Distance only affects who wins the customer.

**Teaching tip:** Use a concrete example: "Your price is $10, distance is 2. Effective price is $12. If you win, you still only get $10 per item."

---

### 10. Bridges — Roads Cross But Don't Connect

Two roads can cross at a bridge without connecting. They remain separate roads. A cart operator on Road A cannot switch to Road B at a bridge.

**Why it confuses people:** Visually it looks like an intersection. In every other game, crossing paths would mean connection.

**Teaching tip:** Point out bridges on the map during setup. Say: "These roads ignore each other. Pretend they're on different floors."

## 🟡 Subtler Gotchas That Emerge Mid-Game

### 11. Entrance Corner Matters for EVERYTHING

The restaurant's Entrance Corner is the starting point for:
- Drink collection routes
- Distance calculations for competition
- Campaign placement range
- Employee action range

A restaurant has only **one** entrance corner (unless a Drive-In is active). Its position relative to roads determines your entire spatial game.

**Why it confuses people:** Players measure from "the restaurant" generically, not from the specific entrance corner. This leads to wrong distance counts and illegal campaign placements.

---

### 12. Campaign Numbering Determines Processing Order

Campaigns are resolved in numerical order during the Marketing phase. **Lower-numbered campaigns go first** and are more likely to successfully place demand (since houses have a 3/5 demand cap).

**Why it confuses people:** Players don't realize that campaign number matters strategically — they just grab any available token. Getting a low-numbered campaign is a real advantage.

---

### 13. Garden Doubles the TOTAL Sale, Not Individual Items

When selling to a house with a garden, you **double the total amount earned from that sale** (item price × items sold × 2). However, milestone bonuses like "First Burger Marketed" (+$5 per burger) are **not** doubled by gardens.

**Why it confuses people:** Players often double each item individually (which gives the same result) but then also try to double the milestone bonuses.

---

### 14. Stock Is Shared Across All Your Restaurants

All food and drink you produce goes into a single shared stock. Any of your restaurants can sell from it. You don't assign items to specific restaurants.

**Why it confuses people:** Thematically, players think each restaurant has its own kitchen/inventory.

---

### 15. Milestones Are Permanent and Sometimes Harmful

Once claimed, milestone effects are **permanent and mandatory**. The "First to Lower Prices" milestone permanently reduces your price by $1 — you can never go back. The "First Billboard Campaign" milestone makes all your future campaigns eternal and keeps Marketeers permanently busy.

**Why it confuses people:** Players see milestones as pure rewards. Some milestones are double-edged swords.

---

### 16. Recruiting Manager / HR Director Unused Actions → Mandatory Salary Discount

If you play a Recruiting Manager and choose not to use their Hire action, that unused action **must** become a $5 salary discount. This is mandatory — you can't choose to ignore it. This matters because it might prevent you from reaching the "$20 in Salaries" milestone.

**Why it confuses people:** The connection between "unused hire action" and "forced salary discount" is non-obvious and buried in the rules.

---

### 17. Mailbox Campaign Reach — Blocked by Roads

Mailbox reach is traced by line-of-sight, but **roads block the line**. Two roads touching at a corner also block it. This is thematic (mail can't cross a road) but deeply unintuitive spatially.

**Why it confuses people:** Players think of mailboxes as area-of-effect. The road-blocking rule is unique to mailboxes and doesn't apply to any other campaign type.

---

### 18. You Cannot Swap Demand Counters on a Full House

Once a house is at its demand limit (3, or 5 with garden), you cannot remove existing demand to make room for new demand. The demand stays until a restaurant serves that house.

**Why it confuses people:** Players want to "overwrite" demand with their own campaign.

---

### 19. Bank Breaking — CEO Slots Can Change

When the bank breaks for the first time, everyone reveals their reserve cards and CEO slot counts may change based on majority vote. Your CEO might go from 3 slots to 2 or 4 — this is a game-altering moment that players often forget about during the intensity of the bank breaking.

**Why it confuses people:** It happens once in the entire game, during an already-complex moment. Players forget about the reserve card mechanic entirely.

---

### 20. Cleanup Phase — ALL Unsold Food Is Discarded

Unless you have the freezer milestone, **everything** in your stock is thrown away at end of round. There's no carryover. Overproducing food is pure waste.

**Why it confuses people:** Players stockpile food "for next round" and are shocked when it all disappears.

---

## 📋 Teaching Priority Order

When teaching FCM, address these confusion points in this order:

1. **Before the game:** #2 (all-or-nothing demand), #5 (marketing timing), #14 (shared stock), #20 (food doesn't carry over)
2. **During setup:** #1 (structure overflow), #6 (turn order), #11 (entrance corner), #19 (bank reserve)
3. **First round:** #8 (salary trap), #7 (mandatory actions), #4 (distance/range/squares)
4. **When relevant:** #3 (road routes), #10 (bridges), #12 (campaign order), #13 (garden doubling), #9 (effective price)
5. **As encountered:** #15 (permanent milestones), #16 (recruiter discount), #17 (mailbox roads), #18 (demand cap)
